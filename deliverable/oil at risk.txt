begin{abstract}
noindent
Crude oil prices act as a key macroeconomic variable, often serving as a primary input in global inflation dynamics, industrial production costs, and international trade balances. The propagation of geopolitical tensions through energy markets can generate heightened volatility, which carries macroeconomic consequences and potential systemic risks for policymakers, firms, and institutional investors. Many traditional econometric approaches assume that the average historical effect of a geopolitical shock adequately represents the underlying stochastic process. However, in the context of crude oil prices and geopolitical shocks, where data tend to exhibit substantial heteroskedasticity, asymmetric responses, and influential tail observations that can distort linear estimates, this assumption may prove overly restrictive.
To address the limitations of conditional mean forecasting, the present research adapts the GaR{} framework to model the distribution of future crude oil price growth. Rather than focusing solely on average baseline effects, the analysis estimates how fluctuations in geopolitical risk shift specific quantiles of the return distribution. This approach aims to capture heterogeneity across outcomes and to describe both downside and upside market risks. The empirical strategy relies on the European Brent crude oil spot price and a dictionary-based Geopolitical Risk Index. Drawing on daily returns and a set of control variables, including a realised-volatility proxy, the U.S. dollar index, and a shipping-cost measure, the study specifies multiple quantile regressions to isolate the tail-risk imprint of geopolitical uncertainty at a one-trading-week horizon. A backtesting framework combining the Kupiec unconditional-coverage test and the dynamic quantile test evaluates the conditional risk measures against realised outcomes, and an entropy-based indicator is examined for early-warning content. The full analytical pipeline is implemented in a self-contained, open-source Python module to promote replicability.
end{abstract}

section{Introduction}
label{secintroduction}

The study of geopolitical risk and its cascading effects on crude oil prices remains a significant concern for the global economy. While inflation dynamics vary due to a multitude of complex macroeconomic factors, oil prices serve as one of their primary drivers, often influencing the cost of global industrial production and affecting international trade balances and current account deficits. Geopolitical shocks, ranging from localised military conflicts and diplomatic standoffs to sweeping international embargoes and maritime blockades, tend to propagate rapidly through energy markets. These events can introduce profound uncertainty that may prompt immediate adjustments in consumption, investment, and precautionary storage among economic agents. Understanding not only whether geopolitical risk affects oil prices, but how exposed future prices might be to the extreme tails of these global risks, is therefore a valuable endeavour for economic stabilisation and financial risk management.

Standard macroeconomic and econometric frameworks have historically tended to favour modelling the conditional mean of asset prices and macroeconomic indicators. By frequently relying on Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression techniques, classical studies often estimate the average historical effect of a geopolitical shock, treating variance as a relatively constant dispersion around that mean. However, the observable dynamics of global oil markets frequently challenge this setup. Energy market data commonly exhibit intense volatility clustering, asymmetric shock responses, and heavy-tailed distributions. In this case, the average effect may be skewed by extremes without adequately capturing them, which can leave policymakers and risk managers with a misleading sense of both the magnitude and the likelihood of adverse price movements.

This empirical challenge aligns with an extensive literature in energy econometrics regarding the potential non-linearities of the oil market. As argued by citet{hamilton2003}, modelling oil shocks often benefits from a flexible approach to account for the possibility that price increases may have a different macroeconomic impact than price decreases. citet{kilian2011} provide evidence suggesting that the macroeconomic response to energy shocks can be asymmetric. Consequently, a strictly linear methodology risks understating downside risks. The exposure of the market may not be adequately captured by mean forecasts; it typically requires a framework specifically equipped to describe asymmetric impacts.

Geopolitical shocks introduce an additional layer of non-linear complexity. citet{pinchetti2024} suggests that large geopolitical shocks raise Brent prices and inflation expectations by triggering precautionary wait-and-see behaviours and a surge in speculative demand for crude. This is further contextualised by citet{blanchardgali2010}, who highlight how modern oil shocks interact differently with the macroeconomy than they did in the 1970s, mediated by factors such as wage rigidities, central bank credibility, and the shifting energy intensity of the economy. The physical materialisation of a threat is not required to damage the economy citet{kilian2026} find that an unanticipated increase in the probability of a major geopolitical oil production shortfall, a perceived disaster scenario, can be sufficient to generate a marked surge in oil prices and macroeconomic uncertainty. The GaR{} methodology, adapted here as OaR{}, offers an econometric solution suited to this dynamic. By isolating specific quantiles, it describes how surges in geopolitical uncertainty fatten the tails of the distribution, allowing for a nuanced analysis of tail behaviour that linear models frequently miss.

To quantify these tail risks conditional on changes in geopolitical and macroeconomic regressors, the empirical procedure is structured in sequential steps. Rather than collapsing the complexity of the oil market into a single conditional mean forecast, the analysis begins by estimating multiple quantile regressions at selected points of the conditional distribution, from the lower tail through the median to the upper tail, so that the model can reveal how geopolitical risk influences both the median and the extremes. The raw output from the quantile regressions provides a discrete inverse cumulative distribution function, which is then smoothed by fitting a flexible continuous distribution. Once the continuous conditional distribution is built, the final step evaluates the magnitude of the upside and downside risks, describing how exposed the baseline forecast might be to unexpected shocks. This exposure is measured using Value-at-Risk, Expected Shortfall, Kullback--Leibler divergence, and distributional skewness, each capturing a distinct dimension of the risk profile.

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows. Section~ref{secliterature} reviews the relevant literature. Section~ref{seccontributions} sets out the contributions. Section~ref{secdata} describes the data. Section~ref{secmethodology} develops the methodological framework, covering quantile regression, the direct forecasting design, the CAViaR augmentation, distributional fitting via the Johnson $S_U$, and the suite of risk and vulnerability metrics. Section~ref{secempirical} presents the empirical implementation and out-of-sample results. Section~ref{secews} studies the entropy-based early warning indicator. Section~ref{secconclusion} concludes. The appendices collect the full technical development of every diagnostic and evaluation test, and a Graphical Annex displays the figures referenced throughout.

section{Background and Literature Review}
label{secliterature}

The methodological foundation of this research builds upon the Vulnerable Growth paradigm introduced by citet{adrian2019}. By modelling the conditional distribution of gross domestic product growth as a function of economic and financial conditions, their framework observed that deteriorating financial conditions tend to increase conditional volatility and shift the lower quantiles downward, while upper quantiles remain comparatively stable. This paradigm indicated that downside risks exhibit significant variance based on financial tightness, highlighting the limitations of conditional mean estimation and the value of evaluating tail risks. Extending this rationale suggests that similar non-linear, tail-heavy dynamics might govern other critical macroeconomic variables that are frequently subjected to sudden, severe shocks.

The recent literature has adapted this tail-risk perspective to the effects of geopolitical uncertainty on energy markets. citet{brignone2025} examine the non-linear transmission mechanisms of geopolitical risk shocks, suggesting that large positive shocks can trigger a disproportionate amplification channel driven by rising uncertainty. By decomposing geopolitical risk, they observe that geopolitical acts often function similarly to negative demand shocks, whereas geopolitical threats tend to behave as positive uncertainty shocks that elevate oil prices and inflation expectations. Similarly, citet{verduzco2026} use high-frequency data to isolate geopolitical oil price shocks, finding that these events typically resemble severe supply disruptions, inducing immediate production declines and sharp price increases. They further note that these shocks are coupled with heightened uncertainty that prompts a distinct precautionary inventory response, characterised by an initial surge in inventories followed by a gradual drawdown as the immediate geopolitical threat recedes. Their work reinforces the view that geopolitical risk does not merely shift the conditional mean; it reshapes the distribution of future oil price outcomes.

The quantile regression methodology was proposed by citet{koenker1978}, who introduced the estimation of conditional quantile functions as an alternative to the conditional mean. Their approach provides a distribution-agnostic estimator that is robust to outliers and capable of revealing heterogeneous covariate effects across the conditional distribution. In the context of risk management, quantile regression acquired a dynamic dimension through the Conditional Autoregressive Value-at-Risk (CAViaR) framework of citet{engle2004}, which allows the conditional quantile to depend autoregressively on its own past values and on past violations, thereby capturing the persistence and clustering of tail events that static models miss.

The distributional fitting step of the GaR{} framework has traditionally relied on the Azzalini skewed-$t$ distribution, as in citet{adrian2019}. However, the choice of parametric family is not innocuous. The Johnson $S_U$ distribution, introduced by citet{johnson1949}, offers an alternative with independent control over skewness and kurtosis through two shape parameters, and a closed-form quantile function that eliminates the need for numerical root-finding. These properties make it suited to modelling commodity returns, where the direction and magnitude of tail asymmetry shift as geopolitical conditions evolve. The $S_U$ family has been used in portfolio management and Value-at-Risk contexts, and its application to the distributional step of a GaR{}-type framework is, to our knowledge, uncommon in the energy-finance literature.

The backtesting of conditional risk measures draws on a well-established body of work. citet{kupiec1995} proposed the unconditional coverage test that compares the empirical frequency of Value-at-Risk exceedances against the nominal rate. The dynamic quantile test of citet{engle2004} provides a stringent evaluation by testing whether the hit sequence is a martingale difference sequence, orthogonal to its own past. For Expected Shortfall, the backtesting framework of citet{acerbi2014,acerbi2017} exploits the joint elicitability of VaR and ES, constructing tests based on the severity of exceedances rather than merely their frequency. The scoring-rule theory of citet{gneiting2007} provides the grounding for strictly proper scoring rules used in model comparison. Together, these tools form the backtesting apparatus applied in this thesis. The information-theoretic component of the framework rests on citet{shannon1948} and the relative-entropy measure of citet{kullback1951}.

% 3. CONTRIBUTIONS

section{Contributions}
label{seccontributions}

This thesis makes the following contributions to the literature on commodity market risk and econometric methodology.

First, it introduces the concept of OaR{} (OaR), adapting the GaR{} (GaR) framework of citet{adrian2019} to the crude oil market. The adaptation enables probabilistic tail-risk assessments of oil prices in the same language used for macrofinancial surveillance.

Second, it integrates Conditional Autoregressive Value-at-Risk (CAViaR) ideas citep{engle2004} into the OaR framework. This enrichment supplements the static quantile regression step of the canonical GaR procedure with variables that encode the model's own recent boundary violations, aiming to capture the persistence and asymmetric adjustment of tail risk observed in oil price behaviour.

Third, it departs from the conventional use of the skewed-$t$ distribution for distributional fitting by adopting the Johnson $S_U$ (JSU) family citep{johnson1949}. The JSU system provides a four-parameter characterisation capable of matching a range of skewness and kurtosis combinations. The skewed-$t$ remains the reference in the canonical GaR{} framework citep{adrian2019}; it is not estimated here, and no head-to-head comparison between the two families is attempted.

Fourth, it examines an entropy-based early warning indicator that exploits daily data. The indicator is the Kullback--Leibler divergence citep{kullback1951} of the fitted conditional density against a reference density, decomposed by tail. Its anticipatory content is assessed with the Cross-Correlation Function and the Granger causality test citep{granger1969}, rather than being assumed.

Fifth, it applies a common backtesting battery uniformly across all model variants. Kupiec unconditional coverage tests citep{kupiec1995} and Diebold--Mariano comparisons of predictive accuracy citep{diebold1995} are applied to every specification, with the dynamic quantile test citep{engle2004} used to assess the hit-sequence dynamics of the CAViaR-augmented models.

Sixth, it delivers a self-contained, open-source Python module that implements the full OaR pipeline, covering data preprocessing, quantile regression under both standard and CAViaR-augmented specifications, distributional fitting, entropy computation, and backtesting diagnostics. The module is publicly available in the following repository at url{httpsgithub.comAlejandro-bot-oneOil-at-Risk} to support replication and extension.

section{Data Description}
label{secdata}

The dependent variable is the European Brent crude oil spot price (USD per barrel,
U.S. Energy Information Administration via FRED), sampled daily from 2 January
1990 to 30 April 2026, a window spanning numerous major geopolitical crises
(Figure 1). Brent is the standard global benchmark, reflecting
international seaborne market conditions. Prices are transformed into daily
log-returns, and weekends and market holidays are removed to keep a continuous
trading-day alignment. The sample median daily log-return is close to zero,
consistent with the near-martingale behaviour of high-frequency commodity
returns; this matters below, because it implies the median quantile carries
little directional signal and most of the conditioning information is expected in
the tails.

The main explanatory variable is the daily Geopolitical Risk Index (GPRD), built by Caldara and Iacovello (2018) as a
dictionary-based measure of the share of newspaper coverage devoted to adverse
geopolitical events. It is entered as a seven-day moving average, matching the
calendar-week basis on which the index is published
(Section~ref{subsecspecification}); country-specific versions allow localised
risk origins to be examined.

Three exogenous controls isolate the geopolitical effect. The U.S. Dollar Index
(log-differenced) captures the dollar--oil link since Brent is priced in dollars,
an appreciation tends to depress its nominal price, and controlling for it
prevents exchange-rate moves from being read as geopolitical shocks. Realised
volatility controls for volatility clustering and heteroskedasticity, separating
the structural pricing of geopolitical threat from general market turbulence; its
handling differs between the in-sample and out-of-sample exercises
(Sections~ref{subsecspecification} and~ref{subsecdirect}). The Baltic Dry
Index (log-differenced), the cost of shipping bulk raw materials by sea, proxies
global industrial demand and controls for demand-side pressures largely free of
speculative noise.

section{Methodology}
label{secmethodology}

When examining the effect of geopolitical risk on the rate of change of Brent crude prices, traditional linear methodologies reveal their limitations. An initial look at the data displays a high presence of influential observations, data points located far outside the dense central cloud that can heavily distort OLS regressions. Because OLS minimises the sum of squared residuals, it is highly sensitive to these extreme outliers, which inflate the variance of the estimates and can bias the slope in either direction, so that the fitted mean relationship offers little reliable predictive power in the tails. Linear methods focus entirely on the expected value or conditional mean, ignoring parts of the distribution such as the tails. The GaR{} methodology, adapted here as OaR{}, provides a direct econometric alternative. By isolating specific quantiles, it describes how surges in geopolitical uncertainty fatten the tails of the distribution, enabling a nuanced analysis of tail behaviour that linear models miss.


subsection{Quantile Regression}
label{subsecqr}

While standard OLS regression provides a powerful tool for estimating the Conditional Expectation Function, it operates under strict assumptions and is highly sensitive to outliers. Quantile regression, introduced by citet{koenker1978}, estimates the Conditional Quantile Function (CQF). Instead of modelling the mean, it models a specific quantile $tau$ (for instance, the 10th or 90th percentiles), making it distribution-agnostic and robust to extreme outliers.

The CQF, given a vector of regressors $X_i$, is the inverse of the conditional cumulative distribution
begin{equation}
Q_tau(Y_i mid X_i) = F_Y^{-1}(tau mid X_i).
label{eqcqf}
end{equation}

Just as OLS derives the conditional mean by minimising the expected squared error, quantile regression derives the CQF by minimising an expected asymmetric loss
begin{equation}
Q_tau(Y_i mid X_i) = argmin_{q(X_i)} , mathbb{E}!left[rho_tau!left(Y_i - q(X_i)right)right],
label{eqqr_objective}
end{equation}
where $rho_tau(u)$ is the check function. To trace out specific percentiles of a distribution, the regression must penalise under-predictions and over-predictions asymmetrically. If we are estimating the 90th quantile ($tau = 0.90$), the model should heavily penalise under-predictions (negative residuals) and lightly penalise over-predictions (positive residuals). To achieve this while maintaining the convexity required for optimisation, a piecewise linear function based on the absolute error is constructed. Multiplying the positive piece by the target quantile $tau$ and the negative piece by $(1 - tau)$ introduces the necessary asymmetry. The check function is written as
begin{equation}
rho_tau(u) = u cdot bigl(tau - mathbf{1}_{u  0}bigr),
label{eqcheck}
end{equation}
where $mathbf{1}_{u0}$ is an indicator function taking the value~1 if the residual is negative and~0 otherwise. Defining the residual as $u_t = y_t - x_t'beta_tau$, the objective is to find the parameter vector $hatbeta_tau$ that minimises the sum of this asymmetric check function across all observed periods
begin{equation}
hatbeta_tau = argmin_{beta_tau in mathbb{R}^k} sum_{t=1}^{T} rho_tau!left(y_t - x_t'beta_tauright).
label{eqqr_estimator}
end{equation}

The economic appeal of this estimator is immediate. In the context of crude oil markets, OLS would estimate a single slope linking geopolitical risk to average returns. By contrast, quantile regression estimates a separate slope at each quantile level, revealing whether geopolitical shocks predominantly compress the lower tail (raising downside risk), stretch the upper tail (amplifying upside potential), or shift the entire distribution. This heterogeneity across quantiles is the identifying premise of the OaR{} framework. The optimisation procedure, which transforms the non-differentiable check function into a standard linear programme, is detailed in Appendix~ref{secappendix_optim}.


subsection{Empirical Specification}
label{subsecspecification}

The empirical specification retained for in-sample estimation regresses daily Brent crude oil log-returns on a seven-day moving average of the Geopolitical Risk Index and a vector of control variables. For each quantile in the target set $tau in {0.01, 0.05, 0.25, 0.50, 0.75, 0.95, 0.99}$, the empirical equation is
begin{equation}
Q_tau(Deltatext{Brent}_t) = beta_0(tau) + beta_1(tau),text{GPRD-MA7}_{t} + gamma(tau)' X_t,
label{eqempirical}
end{equation}
where the in-sample control vector is
begin{equation}
X_t = bigl(Deltatext{USDI}_t,; text{RV}_{t-1},; Deltatext{BDI}_tbigr)'.
label{eqcontrols}
end{equation}
A single specification is imposed uniformly across all quantiles to preserve comparability across the conditional distribution and to prevent tail-specific overfitting. The estimated coefficient paths across $tau$, together with the OLS benchmark, are displayed in Figure 3}.

The primary regressor is a seven-day moving average of the Geopolitical Risk Index, chosen to match a calendar week of the underlying index, which is compiled on a calendar-week basis (seven calendar days)
begin{equation}
text{GPRD-MA7}_t = frac{1}{7}sum_{j=0}^{6}text{GPRD}_{t-j}.
label{eqgprd_ma7}
end{equation}
The seven-day moving average is used to capture the transmission of geopolitical risk through the news cycle, which propagates to market positioning over several days rather than within a single session, so that $hatbeta_1(tau)$ reflects sustained geopolitical stress rather than the transient spike of an individual headline. The seven-day length is a deliberately simple, arbitrary choice made on this rationale; it is not optimised, and alternative smoothing windows are entirely admissible and would be a natural robustness dimension for future work.

Realised volatility enters the in-sample regression at lag $t-1$ rather than contemporaneously, as a deliberate precaution against look-ahead bias. Because realised volatility at time $t$ is computed from intraperiod price movements, it is partly determined by the dependent variable itself, which would induce a simultaneity problem in a contemporaneous ($h=1$) regression. Using $text{RV}_{t-1}$ ensures the regressor is fully observable before period $t$ opens. This concern is specific to the in-sample specification, in which the target and the volatility proxy share the same date. In the out-of-sample direct forecasting exercise of Section~ref{subsecdirect}, where the target is the return realised $h$ trading days ahead, contemporaneous realised volatility $text{RV}_t$ is already part of the information set available at the forecast origin and cannot leak future information; the lag is therefore unnecessary there, and $text{RV}_t$ is used.footnote{Equivalently in the in-sample equation the target $y_t$ and $text{RV}_t$ share the same date, so entering $text{RV}_t$ would place a function of the dependent variable on the right-hand side and induce simultaneity; the one-period lag removes it. In the direct-forecasting equation the target is $y_{t+h}$ with $hgeq 1$, so $text{RV}_t$ is dated strictly before the target and belongs to the origin-$t$ information set, carrying no look-ahead. The two specifications thus use $text{RV}_{t-1}$ and $text{RV}_{t}$ respectively for the same underlying reason.}

Equation (5) represents a parsimonious specification selected from a broader set of candidate models evaluated during the model-selection stage. It is presented primarily as a descriptive benchmark to illustrate how geopolitical risk affects different regions of the conditional distribution of Brent returns while controlling for the main sources of contemporaneous market conditions. Although alternative specifications were also considered during model development, equation (5) provides a framework that serves as a natural starting point for the forecasting methodology developed below. In Section 5.4, this baseline specification is adapted to the direct forecasting setting, where the same conditioning variables are used to model the conditional distribution of future Brent returns.

Finally, the candidate specifications were also screened for multicollinearity using standard diagnostic measures (see, for instance,  Table 1), and no evidence of problematic collinearity was found among the retained regressors.

subsection{Diagnosis of the Quantile Regression}
label{subsecdiagnosis}

The diagnostic battery provides broad empirical support for the validity of specification~eqref{eqempirical}. The Koenker--Machado $R^1(tau)$ citep{koenker1999} indicates that the inclusion of the regressors yields a meaningful reduction in the weighted objective function relative to the intercept-only benchmark, with explanatory power concentrated in the tail quantiles, precisely where the OaR{} metric is most informative. This concentration is expected rather than anomalous the conditioning variables are designed to describe extreme events, and, because the median daily return is close to zero and carries little directional signal, the regressors add proportionally more at the tails than at the centre. The QAR unit root test of citet{koenker2004} rejects the null of quantile-specific non-stationarity at all targeted quantiles, consistent with mean-reverting behaviour of Brent log-returns throughout the conditional distribution. The Wald test for equality of slopes across quantiles rejects the null of coefficient constancy, supporting the view that the regressors exert heterogeneous effects across the conditional distribution, which is the identifying premise of the OaR{} framework. No quantile crossing violations are detected in the fitted paths, consistent with the monotonicity requirement across all observations in the sample. The complete technical development of each diagnostic, with its null hypothesis, test statistic, and asymptotic distribution, is provided in Appendix~ref{secappendix_diagnostics}.


subsection{Direct Multi-Step Forecasting}
label{subsecdirect}

The out-of-sample analysis is genuinely predictive it targets the conditional
distribution of Brent returns $h$ periods ahead using only information available
today. The iterated approach---chaining a one-step model forward---is unavailable
here, because conditional quantiles do not obey a law of iterated expectations a
multi-step quantile cannot be recovered by composing one-step quantile forecasts
the way a conditional mean can.

The analysis therefore uses direct forecasting a separate quantile regression is
estimated for each horizon $h$ by pairing the information available at $t$ with
the realised future value $Y_{t+h}$,
begin{equation}
hatbeta^{(h)}(tau) = argmin_{beta} sum_{t=1}^{T-h} rho_tau!left(Y_{t+h} - X_t'betaright),quad Q_tau(Y_{t+h} mid X_t) = X_t'beta^{(h)}(tau).
label{eqdirect}
end{equation}
Because a dedicated model is fitted at each horizon, the estimator targets the
horizon directly and avoids the compounding bias of the recursive scheme;
re-estimating over a grid of $h$ also shows how the geopolitical risk premium
propagates over time. This is also the setting in which contemporaneous realised
volatility $text{RV}_t$ can be used without look-ahead bias
(Section~ref{subsecspecification}), since the target lies $h$ days beyond the
forecast origin.

Estimation and evaluation are kept strictly apart at each origin the model
trains on the rolling window $[t-1000,, t)$ (about four trading years), issues
one $h$-step forecast, and advances one day, sweeping every eligible origin. The
realised returns against the forecasted quantile envelope are shown in
Figure 4}, and the in-sample forecast around a stressed episode in
Figure 10}.

subsection{Competing Specifications Capturing Geopolitical Tail Risk}
label{subseccaviar}

The standard quantile regression is an accurate description of the typical conditional quantile, but it is, in a precise sense, memoryless about its own failures. It maps today's geopolitical and financial covariates into a quantile forecast and then forgets whether that forecast was breached. In tranquil markets this is harmless; in the tails it is not.

The economic object of interest is not the centre of the distribution but its extreme edge, the threshold beyond which an oil position suffers a large loss. Extreme oil moves are not independent draws they cluster. A supply shock threatening the Strait of Hormuz, or a demand collapse of the COVID-19 type, does not resolve in a single session; it propagates through inventory decisions, futures-curve repositioning, and risk re-pricing over days and weeks. Empirically this shows up as volatility clustering in the tail, where a Value-at-Risk breach today materially raises the probability of another breach tomorrow. A model that treats each tail event as an isolated surprise is therefore caught off guard precisely when accuracy matters most.

This is the gap that citet{engle2004} address with the CAViaR framework, in which the conditional quantile is allowed to depend autoregressively on its own past behaviour, so that the tail remembers recent stress. The present thesis does not estimate a full CAViaR recursion; instead it imports the central insight into the direct-forecasting design by augmenting the standard specification with variables that encode the model's own recent boundary violations. Two augmentation variants are considered.

subsubsection{Model 2 CAViaR Indicator Augmentation}

The first variant appends a pair of binary stress flags to the regressor set. Let $hat{Q}^{text{high}}_{smid s-h}$ and $hat{Q}^{text{low}}_{smid s-h}$ denote the upper and lower boundary quantiles (the $0.95$ and $0.05$ marks) forecast for date $s$ from information dated $s-h$. The flags evaluate whether the realised return breached that pre-committed boundary
begin{align}
text{upside_breach}_{t} &= mathbf{1}!bigl(y_{t}  hat{Q}^{text{high}}_{tmid t-h}bigr), label{eqbreach_up}[4pt]
text{downside_breach}_{t} &= mathbf{1}!bigl(y_{t}  hat{Q}^{text{low}}_{tmid t-h}bigr). label{eqbreach_down}
end{align}
Both flags are dated $t$ and depend only on quantities observable by $t$ the realised return $y_{t}$ and a boundary that was itself fixed at $t-h$. When these regressors enter the forecast of the future target $y_{t+h}$ formed at origin $t$, every ingredient is therefore dated at or before $t$, strictly earlier than the target, so no future information is used.footnote{Concretely, the regressor vector at origin $t$ contains $text{breach}_{t}$, which is a function of $y_{t}$ and of the boundary $hat{Q}_{tmid t-h}$ committed $h$ days earlier; the object being predicted is $y_{t+h}$. The availability lag between the most recent breach information and the target is thus at least one period and the flag can never read the realised value it is helping to forecast.} A positive coefficient on these indicators means that the model widens its predicted quantile corridor after a recent breach, absorbing the persistence that a memoryless specification would miss.

subsubsection{Model 3 CAViaR Severity Augmentation}

A binary flag records that a breach happened but is blind to its violence a 1% breach and a 10% breach speak with the same voice. Yet for tail risk the magnitude is much of the story, the difference between a manageable loss and a blow-up. The severity variant therefore replaces the indicators with the absolute size of the exceedance, the fallout error
begin{align}
text{upside_severity}_{t} &= max!bigl(0,; y_{t} - hat{Q}_{tmid t-h}^{text{high}}bigr), label{eqsev_up}[4pt]
text{downside_severity}_{t} &= max!bigl(0,; hat{Q}_{tmid t-h}^{text{low}} - y_{t}bigr), label{eqsev_down}
end{align}
Entering the fallout error in absolute value forces its coefficient to act as a pure stress-intensity loading, uncontaminated by the sign of the exceedance, so the upper- and lower-tail severities read as independent measures of how hard the market overshot. Because both augmentations need a realised value to define a breach, the stress signal they carry is sharp at very short horizons but decays as $h$ lengthens, an economic limitation the out-of-sample results make concrete.

subsubsection{The Dynamic Quantile Test and the Role of Breach Memory}
label{subsecdq_caviar}

The motivation for these augmentations is made precise by the Dynamic Quantile (DQ) test of citet{engle2004}, the sharpest available check on whether a quantile model has absorbed the dynamics of its own violations. Define the centred hit sequence
begin{equation}
text{Hit}_t(tau) = mathbf{1}!bigl(y_t  hat{q}_t(tau)bigr) - tau,
label{eqhit}
end{equation}
which has mean zero under correct specification because a well-calibrated $tau$-quantile is breached exactly a fraction $tau$ of the time. Correct emph{dynamic} specification requires more than the right average it requires that a breach today be unpredictable from anything known yesterday, so that the hit sequence is a martingale difference with respect to the information set $Omega_{t-1}$,
begin{equation}
H_0; mathbb{E}!left[text{Hit}_t(tau)mid Omega_{t-1}right] = 0 .
label{eqdq_null}
end{equation}
The test operationalises this by projecting the hits onto an instrument matrix $Z_t$ built from a constant, $L$ lagged hits $text{Hit}_{t-1}(tau),dots,text{Hit}_{t-L}(tau)$, and the lagged fitted quantile $hat{q}_{t-1}(tau)$, and testing the joint significance of the projection through the Wald form
begin{equation}
DQ(tau) = frac{widehat{text{Hit}}'Z,(Z'Z)^{-1}Z'widehat{text{Hit}}}{tau(1-tau)} ;xrightarrow{d}; chi^2_{q},
label{eqdq}
end{equation}
with $q$ equal to the column dimension of $Z$; the equivalent Lagrange-multiplier form $Ncdot R^2$ from the auxiliary regression converges to the same law. Rejection means the violation process is still predictable, i.e. the model is caught off guard by clustered tail events. The two CAViaR augmentations attack exactly the term the DQ test interrogates by feeding the model's own lagged breach information back into the quantile forecast, they are constructed so that recent violations no longer carry predictive information for the next one. In that sense the CAViaR-augmented specifications pass the DQ test by construction, since they internalise precisely the lagged-hit dependence that the statistic is designed to detect.


subsection{Distribution Fitting From Discrete Quantile Forecasts to a Continuous Conditional Density}
label{subsecdistribution}

The direct forecasting model delivers, for every forecast origin $t$ and horizon $h$, a finite set of predicted conditional quantiles
begin{equation}
hat{Q}_{t+h} = bigl{hat{q}_{t+h}(tau_1),; ldots,; hat{q}_{t+h}(tau_K)bigr},quad tau_k in {0.01,, 0.05,, 0.25,, 0.50,, 0.75,, 0.95,, 0.99}.
label{eqquantile_skeleton}
end{equation}
These point forecasts are the discrete skeleton of the conditional distribution of future Brent returns. A portfolio manager who needs only a single Value-at-Risk threshold at the 5th percentile can read $hat{q}_{t+h}(0.05)$ directly and stop. But we would like to extend the OaR{} framework well beyond a single threshold.

Consider what a central bank or a sovereign wealth fund actually needs when a geopolitical crisis unfolds in the Persian Gulf. The question is not merely whether the 5th percentile of the return distribution has moved; it is how the entire shape of the conditional density has changed. Has the left tail fattened while the right tail remained stable, signalling a pure downside tail risk Has skewness reversed, as it does when a demand-side collapse overtakes a supply-side fear To compute Expected Shortfall, one must integrate below the quantile. To measure Kullback--Leibler divergence between successive conditional densities, one must have a density. To study how geopolitical shocks reshape the skewness and kurtosis of the forecast distribution across horizons, one must recover the full shape of the curve, not merely a handful of points on it. The discrete quantile skeleton must therefore be interpolated into a continuous parametric density that preserves the information encoded in the quantile forecasts while supplying the analytic tractability that downstream tail-risk metrics require.

subsubsection{The Johnson $S_U$ Distribution}



The discrete quantile skeleton is interpolated with the Johnson $S_U$
distribution, a four-parameter family $Theta = (xi, lambda, gamma, delta)$
obtained by applying an inverse-hyperbolic-sine transformation to a standard
normal. It is chosen for two reasons. First, its two shape parameters give
independent control over skewness ($gamma$) and tail weight ($delta$), so it
can track the asymmetric tail rotations of crude oil returns,right-tail
fattening under supply threats, left-tail fattening under demand collapses,and
it nests the Gaussian in the limit $gamma to 0, delta to infty$. Second, its
cumulative distribution function admits a closed-form inverse, so the quantile
function can be evaluated without numerical root-finding, which makes the Minimum
Distance Estimation step below as fast as a least-squares problem. The formal
definition and quantile function are given in
Appendix~ref{secappendix_jsu}.


subsubsection{Minimum Distance Estimation}

In the classical Maximum Likelihood setting, the analyst observes many realisations from the return-generating process, each an independent draw from the stochastic process whose density is to be estimated. The likelihood surface is well identified, the sample is large, and the estimator is efficient. The setting that arises after the quantile regression step is different. For a given forecast origin $t$ and horizon $h$, we do not observe a large sample of future returns. We observe a small vector of $K$ predicted quantiles, one for each targeted quantile level. With $K = 7$, we have seven data points, not seven thousand. The natural estimator in this setting is Minimum Distance Estimation (MDE), a technique that estimates the parameters of a distribution by minimising a measure of discrepancy between its theoretical quantile function and a set of target quantiles (here, the predicted quantiles), rather than by maximising a likelihood over raw observations. Concretely, the MDE finds the parameter vector $Theta = (xi, lambda, gamma, delta)$ that makes the theoretical quantile function of the chosen distribution pass as close as possible to the predicted quantiles
begin{equation}
hatTheta_t = argmin_{Theta} sum_{k=1}^{K}!bigl[hat{q}_{t+h}(tau_k) - Q_{text{JSU}}(tau_k;,Theta)bigr]^2.
label{eqmde}
end{equation}


subsubsection{Evaluation The Probability Integral Transform}

Once the MDE has produced a fitted conditional distribution for every forecast origin, the critical question becomes whether those distributions are accurate descriptions of the future reality they purport to model. The foundational diagnostic is the Probability Integral Transform (PIT), formalised in the context of density forecast evaluation by citet{diebold1998}. For each forecast origin $t$ in the out-of-sample window, the PIT evaluates the realised return $y_{t+h}$ under the conditional CDF that was forecasted for that date
begin{equation}
u_t = F!left(y_{t+h} mid hatTheta_tright).
label{eqpit}
end{equation}
If the fitted distribution is the true conditional distribution, the transformed value $u_t$ is exactly Uniform$(0,1)$
begin{equation}
P(U leq u) = u quad text{for all } u in [0,1].
label{eqpit_uniform}
end{equation}
The contrapositive supplies the diagnostic if the sequence of PITs departs from uniformity, the forecasted distribution is misspecified. Departures manifest in characteristic ways that carry direct economic meaning. An excess of PITs near zero indicates that the model's left tail is too thin, understating the probability of large negative returns, precisely the kind of miscalibration that would lead a risk manager to understate her Expected Shortfall during a geopolitical crisis. The PIT histogram and the corresponding diagonal (empirical-versus-theoretical CDF) plot are shown in Figures 12 and 13.

The formal assessment is provided by the Kolmogorov--Smirnov (KS) test citep{massey1951}, which measures the supremum absolute deviation between the empirical CDF of the PIT sequence and the theoretical CDF of the Uniform$(0,1)$ distribution
begin{equation}
D_n = sup_{u in [0,1]} biglhat{F}_n(u) - ubigr.
label{eqks}
end{equation}
The PIT evaluation is conducted over the same out-of-sample window used for the coverage backtests. This design constitutes a single, continuous goodness-of-fit assessment that spans the entire out-of-sample period, evaluating every fitted density across every forecast origin simultaneously. It is more demanding than a pointwise coverage backtest, because it asks whether the entire shape of the conditional density, its location, its spread, its skewness, and its tail weight, is correctly specified at every date. A PIT sequence consistent with uniformity is therefore a necessary condition for treating the fitted distributions as a credible probabilistic characterisation of future oil price tail risks under geopolitical duress.


subsection{Risk and Vulnerability Metrics}
label{subsecrisk_metrics}

Having recovered a continuous conditional Johnson $S_U$ density at every forecast origin, the framework extracts the risk and vulnerability metrics that constitute the diagnostic output of the OaR{} framework. A density, by itself, is not a decision tool. A risk manager facing a potential supply disruption in the Strait of Hormuz does not consult a probability density function; she asks how large her worst-case loss might be, how severe the tail would be if that worst case materialises, and whether the current distribution has shifted in a way that signals an abnormal accumulation of downside risk relative to historical norms. These are different questions, and they require different metrics.

subsubsection{Value-at-Risk}

Value-at-Risk is the $alpha$-quantile of the conditional return distribution. For the left tail
begin{equation}
text{VaR}^{text{Left}}_{t+h}(alpha) = Q_alpha!left(r_{t+h} mid hatTheta_tright),
label{eqvar_left}
end{equation}
and for the right tail
begin{equation}
text{VaR}^{text{Right}}_{t+h}(1-alpha) = Q_{1-alpha}!left(r_{t+h} mid hatTheta_tright),
label{eqvar_right}
end{equation}
where $text{VaR}^{text{Left}}  0$ represents a loss threshold and $text{VaR}^{text{Right}}  0$ represents a gain threshold. In plain terms, the left-tail measure reports the return the market would realise under a very adverse scenario, that is, a return so low that it is expected to be exceeded on the downside only with probability $alpha$; the right-tail measure reports the return under a very favourable scenario, so high that it is expected to be exceeded on the upside only with probability $alpha$. Computing both is essential because the conditional distribution fitted by the Johnson $S_U$ is generically asymmetric when the Geopolitical Risk Index is elevated and the dollar is weakening, the two tails need not move in parallel, and reporting only the left tail would discard the information contained in the right tail's response to the same conditioning variables.

The conditional VaR follows directly from the fitted density
begin{equation}
text{VaR}^{text{Cond, Left}}_{t+h}(alpha) = hatxi_t + hatlambda_t sinh!left(frac{Phi^{-1}(alpha) - hatgamma_t}{hatdelta_t}right).
label{eqvar_jsu}
end{equation}
A useful observation is that no time-scaling is needed for the conditional VaR. The quantile regression that produced the inputs to the distribution fitting step was estimated with the target variable $y_{t+h}$, the return over the next $h$ trading days. The fitted Johnson $S_U$ distribution is therefore already a distribution of $h$-step-ahead returns. Its $alpha$-quantile is directly the $h$-day VaR, without any square-root-of-time adjustment.

The unconditional VaR, computed by historical simulation over a rolling window of $W = 1{,}000$ observations, serves as the benchmark against which the conditional measure is assessed. When the two VaR series track each other closely, the conditioning variables are adding little information beyond what the recent history already contains. When they diverge, the conditional model is detecting a shift in conditions that the backward-looking historical measure has not yet absorbed. The conditional and unconditional VaR and CVaR series are displayed in Figure 15 and 16.

subsubsection{Expected Shortfall}

Value-at-Risk answers how bad a loss can be at a given confidence level, but it is silent on what happens beyond that threshold. Two distributions can share the same 97.5th percentile while having radically different tail behaviour beyond it one may decay rapidly to zero, implying that exceedances beyond the VaR are rare and mild, while the other may exhibit a heavy tail extending far into the loss region, implying that when the VaR is breached, the resulting loss is likely to be large. In crude oil markets, this distinction is not hypothetical. The distribution of returns during the COVID-19 demand collapse of April 2020 had a left tail extending to losses of 30 percent or more in a single day, while the distribution during the preceding months of gradual price decline had a much thinner left tail despite a similar VaR at the 2.5th percentile.

Expected Shortfall addresses this limitation by measuring the average loss in the tail beyond the VaR threshold
begin{equation}
text{ES}^{text{Left}}_{t+h}(alpha) = mathbb{E}!left[r_{t+h} mid r_{t+h} leq text{VaR}^{text{Left}}_{t+h}(alpha)right].
label{eqes}
end{equation}
The left-tail Expected Shortfall is always more negative than the left-tail VaR, because the conditional expectation in the tail lies beyond the tail's boundary by construction. The difference $text{ES}^{text{Left}} - text{VaR}^{text{Left}}$ measures the expected severity of a VaR exceedance, a quantity of direct interest to a risk manager who has already accepted that the VaR threshold may be breached and wants to know how bad the outcome is likely to be.

Expected Shortfall enjoys a property that Value-at-Risk does not it is a coherent risk measure in the sense of citet{artzner1999}. The critical property is sub-additivity, which was one of the principal motivations behind the Basel Committee's decision, in the Fundamental Review of the Trading Book, to replace VaR with Expected Shortfall as the basis for internal-model capital calculations under Basel III citep{bcbs2019}.

When the conditional density is available in closed form, the Expected Shortfall integral is evaluated by numerical integration on a fine grid of 5{,}000 points spanning the support of the distribution
begin{equation}
text{ES}^{text{Cond, Left}}_{t+h}(alpha) approx frac{displaystylesum_{x_i leq text{VaR}^{text{Left}}} x_i, f(x_i mid hatTheta_t),Delta x}{displaystylesum_{x_i leq text{VaR}^{text{Left}}} f(x_i mid hatTheta_t),Delta x}.
label{eqes_numerical}
end{equation}

subsubsection{Kullback--Leibler Divergence as a Vulnerability Indicator}

Value-at-Risk and Expected Shortfall are point statistics read off the
conditional density at specific tail probabilities; they do not measure how far
the whole density has moved from a reference state. Kullback--Leibler divergence
citep{kullback1951} supplies that global measure of distributional distance. It
is non-negative, zero only when the two densities coincide, and asymmetric in an
economically meaningful way it penalises a reference that treats as
near-impossible the large losses the conditional forecast deems plausible---the
dangerous case for a risk manager. Following citet{adrian2019}, it is decomposed
into left- and right-tail components at the tail mark $tau = 0.95$ and measured
against two baselines a Normal density (a kurtosis-sensitive early-warning
signal of departure from Gaussianity) and the unconditional Johnson $S_U$ fitted
by Maximum Likelihood (Figure 11), against which a high divergence
signals a genuine shift in the conditional distribution rather than mere
non-normality. The formal definition, its information-theoretic interpretation,
and the tail-decomposition integrals are given in Appendix~ref{secappendix_kl}.

subsubsection{Distributional Skewness}

The Fisher skewness coefficient of the fitted Johnson $S_U$ distribution is computed analytically using the closed-form expressions derived by citet{johnson1949}
begin{equation}
gamma_1 = -frac{sqrt{omega},(omega + 2)left[3sinh!left(frac{gamma}{delta}right) + sinh!left(frac{3gamma}{delta}right)right]}{4left[frac{omega - 1}{2}left(omegacosh!left(frac{2gamma}{delta}right) + 1right)right]^{32}},
label{eqskewness}
end{equation}
where $omega = exp(1delta^2)$. The skewness is negative when the left tail is heavier than the right, signalling an asymmetric accumulation of downside risk, and positive when the right tail dominates. The economic content of time-varying skewness in crude oil markets is substantial. During periods of geopolitical escalation, the right tail fattens relative to the left and skewness becomes positive or less negative. During demand-side recessions, the opposite pattern emerges. Tracking conditional skewness over time reveals the direction in which the balance of risks is tilted at each forecast origin.

subsection{Computational Configuration}
label{subsecconfig}

The risk metrics are computed out-of-sample at every trading day in the test
span, from the same rolling pipeline.

Two out-of-sample spans appear in the thesis, by design rather than
inconsistency. The forecast-evaluation backtests of Section~ref{secempirical}
(pinball, fallout, Kupiec, Diebold--Mariano) are reported over 2 January 2023 to
23 April 2026,$864$ origins for the standard model and $517$ for the CAViaR
variants,a window chosen to keep the coverage counts comparable across the
three specifications. The conditional density, its risk metrics, and the entropy
series of Section~ref{secews} are evaluated over the longer 21 June 2012 to
24 April 2026 span ($2{,}168$ daily values; $2{,}167$ after differencing). Both
are genuine out-of-sample objects from the same pipeline, and each table states
its own sample.

The forecast horizon is $h = 5$ trading days (one trading week), applied
consistently across the forecasting, risk-metric, and early-warning steps.

Two probability levels operate in parallel and should not be conflated. The
quantile forecasts and their coverage and pinball evaluation are assessed at
$tau = 0.95$ (the $0.05$ mark), while the risk measures read from the fitted
density-,VaR and Expected Shortfall,are quoted at $97.5%$ confidence (the
$0.025$ tail), following the Basel III Fundamental Review of the Trading Book
citep{bcbs2019}. The quantile regression is estimated on the grid
$tau in {0.01, 0.05, 0.25, 0.50, 0.75, 0.95, 0.99}$, and the $2.5$th
percentile is read from the fitted Johnson $S_U$ density.

The historical VaR and ES use a rolling window of $W = 1{,}000$ observations
(about four trading years), matching the training window; the quantile-regression
coefficients are refitted every 30 out-of-sample days and cached in between, so
the density still updates daily through the changing covariates while the
coefficient mapping is held fixed between refits.

section{Empirical Implementation and Out-of-Sample Results}

subsection{Evaluating the Forecasts}
label{subsecevaluation}

Three complementary lenses judge the forecasts a strictly proper loss measured over the rolling window, a magnitude-of-exceedance audit, and a formal unconditional-coverage backtest. The mechanics of every test are derived in full in Appendix~ref{secappendix_oos}; here the treatment is kept brief and focused on what each test certifies. All figures below are computed at the operative horizon $h = 5$ and, where a tail is specified, at $tau = 0.95$.

subsubsection{The Pinball Loss and the Generalisation Gap}

A quantile cannot be scored with the symmetric mean-squared error, because over- and under-prediction must be penalised by different amounts. The asymmetric pinball (check) loss supplies that asymmetry
begin{equation}
L_tau(e) = e cdot bigl(tau - mathbf{1}_{e  0}bigr),quad e = Y_{t+h} - hat{y}_{t+ht}(tau).
label{eqpinball}
end{equation}
It is the strictly proper scoring rule for quantiles citep{gneiting2007} it is minimised if and only if the forecast equals the true conditional quantile, so a lower out-of-sample pinball loss is strong evidence of a better tail estimate. Tracking the out-of-sample pinball loss across a dense grid of horizons for each specification reveals how predictive accuracy degrades as the horizon lengthens a loss profile that rises sharply with the horizon is the classic signature of overfitting, whereas a flat profile indicates that the model is capturing structural signal. These per-horizon loss profiles are shown in Figure 5. In the present experiment the benchmark's loss stays broadly flat across the horizons examined, whereas the CAViaR variants deteriorate at longer horizons, consistent with their stale-information handicap.

subsubsection{Fallout (Severity) Audit}

Coverage frequency alone can hide catastrophic single-day failures, so the magnitude of every breach is also audited how far the realised return overshot the predicted 1%--99% tunnel. For risk management, frequent but tiny breaches are far preferable to rare but massive ones, since the latter betray a structural failure to price an impending shock. At $h = 5$ the three models keep the realised return inside the predicted envelope on 97.83% (Standard), 97.17% (CAViaR-indicator) and 97.26% (CAViaR-severity) of test days, with no isolated catastrophic overshoot. The full breach paths for the three models, including the behaviour of the tunnel during the 2020 crisis, are collected in Figures 6,7 and 8.

subsubsection{Coverage Backtests}

Because the model's output is an estimated quantile that functions as a Value-at-Risk threshold, its adequacy is judged with a risk-management backtest built on the binary hit sequence. The citet{kupiec1995} Proportion-of-Failures test checks whether the empirical hit rate $hatpi = xT$ matches the nominal $tau$ through a likelihood-ratio statistic that is asymptotically $chi^2_1$
begin{equation}
LR_{UC} = -2ln!left[frac{(1-tau)^{T-x},tau^{x}}{(1-hatpi)^{T-x},hatpi^{x}}right] sim chi^2_1,qquad hatpi=frac{x}{T}.
label{eqkupiec_main}
end{equation}
The test evaluates unconditional coverage only it asks whether breaches occur at the correct emph{frequency}, and is silent about their timing. This is a deliberate choice in the present setting. Because the $h=5$ direct forecasts overlap, the hit sequence is serially dependent by construction (an MA$(h-1)$ structure), which distorts tests that model the emph{dependence} of breaches, but leaves the expected breach emph{count}, and hence the unconditional-coverage statistic, unaffected.footnote{The overlap inflates the variance of the breach count but not its mean, so the Kupiec point assessment of frequency is robust to the induced serial dependence, whereas an independence-based conditional-coverage test applied naively to the same overlapping sequence would be size-distorted. Conditional-coverage testing is therefore not reported here.} Table~ref{tabcoverage} summarises the results, and the corresponding coverage-convergence plot for the main models is shown in Figure 9.

subsection{Backtesting the Risk Measures}
label{subsecbacktesting}

Risk measures are only as credible as the evidence that they perform as advertised. A Value-at-Risk at the 97.5 percent confidence level is, implicitly, a promise in no more than 2.5 percent of periods should the realised loss exceed the stated threshold. If exceedances occur materially more often, the model is understating risk and the capital reserves it implies are insufficient. If exceedances are materially rarer, the model is overly conservative and the capital it reserves is unnecessarily large, imposing an economic cost in the form of foregone investment.

The framework evaluates the VaR measure with the Kupiec unconditional-coverage test and the Expected Shortfall measure with a joint-elicitability severity test. The Kupiec test counts the number of times the realised return exceeds the VaR threshold and tests whether this count is consistent with the nominal exceedance probability $alpha = 1 - text{confidence}$
begin{equation}
LR_{UC} = -2ln!left[frac{alpha^x(1-alpha)^{n-x}}{hatpi^x(1-hatpi)^{n-x}}right],quad hatpi = frac{x}{n}.
label{eqkupiec}
end{equation}
As in the forecast-evaluation backtests, only unconditional coverage is assessed, because the overlapping $h$-step forecasts induce serial dependence in the hit sequence that would distort an independence-based conditional-coverage test but leaves the frequency assessment intact.

For Expected Shortfall, the citet{acerbi2014,acerbi2017} tests exploit the joint elicitability of VaR and ES, constructing test statistics based on the severity of VaR exceedances rather than merely their frequency. This furnishes a direct check on the depth of the tail beyond the VaR threshold, the quantity that ES is designed to summarise and that a frequency-based VaR backtest cannot see.

Together, these procedures evaluate both the threshold accuracy (VaR) and the tail-severity accuracy (ES) of the OaR{} framework's risk measures. The complete derivation, hypotheses, and test statistics for every backtest are collected in Appendix~ref{secappendix_oos}.

section{Early Warning System}
label{secews}
subsection{Motivation From Vulnerability Measurement to Anticipation}
label{subsecews_motivation}

The risk metrics of the preceding section describe the conditional distribution
at each date; the Kullback--Leibler divergence, in particular, measures how far
it has moved from its reference state. The question this section asks is whether
that movement is predictive if a spike in tail entropy tends to precede large
oil price movements, the measure works not as a coincident diagnostic but as an
early warning signal, giving risk managers a window to adjust hedges and margins
before stress materialises rather than after.

The hypothesis is tested with two independent tools the Cross-Correlation
Function, to see whether entropy leads returns (its peak correlation falling at a
positive lag), and the Granger causality test, to check whether lagged entropy
predicts returns beyond the information already in their own past.
subsection{Choice of the Early Warning Indicator}
label{subsecews_indicator_choice}

Building the indicator requires three choices the reference baseline, the
estimation window, and the forecast horizon.

The baseline is the Normal density, rather than the unconditional Johnson $S_U$
used by citet{adrian2019}. The unconditional $S_U$ fitted to Brent returns is
near-symmetric in its centre but strongly heavy-tailed (excess kurtosis of order
ten; Figure 11), so the KL divergence against a Normal reference
acts as a clean detector of tail fattening near zero in calm periods, rising
sharply when the tails widen under geopolitical or demand-side shocks. Both
references are recalibrated on the data available at each forecast origin, so the
series stays out-of-sample. The two entropy series are shown in
Figures 14 and 15.

The window is rolling, of $W = 1{,}000$ observations (about four trading years),
rather than expanding it reacts faster to changing conditions, is cheaper to
update in real time, and matches the window used for the VaR and Expected
Shortfall computations.

The horizon is $h = 5$ trading days, matching the forecasting and risk-metric
steps shorter horizons leave too little time to act, while longer ones make the
quantile regression, and hence the fitted density, noisier.

The indicator is therefore the rolling-window KL divergence of the conditional
Johnson $S_U$ density against the Normal baseline at $h = 5$, decomposed at the
tail mark $tau = 0.95$ into full-distribution, left-tail (downside), and
right-tail (upside) components.

subsection{The Cross-Correlation Function}
label{subsecews_ccf}

The first diagnostic is the Cross-Correlation Function (CCF), a standard tool for
detecting lead-lag relationships between time series
citep{bujosa2013, brockwell1991}. It reports the correlation between the
indicator at time $t$ and the target at time $t+h$, under the convention that
$h  0$ means the indicator leads the target by $h$ periods. If the entropy
series leads Brent returns, the CCF peaks at a positive lag $h^$, which is then
the anticipation horizon; a peak at $h^ = 0$ (coincident) or $h^  0$ (lagging)
would be of no use for early warning.

Significance at each lag is judged against Bartlett's 95 percent band,
$pm 1.96sqrt{N}$, under the null of independence citep{bartlett1946}. The
estimated function is shown in Figure 17.

Only the entropy indicators are first-differenced before the CCF is computed; the
Brent return enters in levels. The return is already stationary (a log-difference
of prices), so differencing it again would over-difference the series and
attenuate the lead-lag correlations of interest, whereas first-differencing the
entropy series removes its persistent level shifts during prolonged stress while
preserving its lead-lag dynamics.

subsection{Granger Causality}
label{subsecews_granger}

The CCF identifies the lag at which the correlation between two series is maximised, but correlation does not establish predictive content in a rigorous econometric sense. Two series can be highly correlated at a positive lag because they are both driven by a common third factor, without the leading series carrying any incremental predictive information beyond what the target's own history already provides. The Granger causality test citep{granger1969} addresses this limitation by asking a more precise question do lagged values of the entropy indicator improve the forecast of Brent returns beyond the forecast that can be constructed from the returns' own lagged values alone

The test is implemented by comparing two nested linear models. The restricted model regresses the target series on its own lagged values
begin{equation}
label{eqgranger_restricted}
Y_t = alpha_0 + sum_{j=1}^{p} alpha_j Y_{t-j} + e_t,
end{equation}
where $Y_t$ denotes the Brent return at date $t$ in levels (the series is stationary by construction), $p$ is the lag order, $alpha_0$ is an intercept, and $e_t$ is the error term. The unrestricted model augments this specification with lagged values of the entropy indicator
begin{equation}
label{eqgranger_unrestricted}
Y_t = alpha_0 + sum_{j=1}^{p} alpha_j Y_{t-j} + sum_{j=1}^{p} beta_j X_{t-j} + u_t,
end{equation}
where $X_{t-j}$ denotes the first-differenced entropy indicator at lag $j$, and $beta_1, ldots, beta_p$ are the coefficients on the lagged indicator values. The null hypothesis is that the indicator carries no incremental predictive content
begin{equation}
label{eqgranger_null}
H_0 beta_1 = beta_2 = cdots = beta_p = 0.
end{equation}
Under this null, the lagged entropy values are jointly irrelevant for predicting future returns once the returns' own history is accounted for. Rejection of $H_0$ constitutes evidence that the entropy indicator Granger-causes Brent returns, in the specific sense that it carries information about future returns that is not already embedded in the returns' own autoregressive structure.

The test statistic is a standard $F$-test comparing the residual sum of squares of the restricted and unrestricted models
begin{equation}
label{eqgranger_fstat}
F = frac{(text{RSS}_r - text{RSS}_{ur})  p}{text{RSS}_{ur}  (T - 2p - 1)},
end{equation}
where $text{RSS}_r$ and $text{RSS}_{ur}$ are the residual sums of squares of the restricted and unrestricted models respectively, $p$ is the number of lags, and $T$ is the effective sample size after accounting for the lag truncation. Under $H_0$, this statistic follows an $F(p, T - 2p - 1)$ distribution citep{granger1969,hamilton1994}.

The lag order $p$ is selected by the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), which is the standard choice when the primary objective is predictive accuracy rather than model parsimony citep{akaike1974}. The AIC balances goodness of fit against model complexity by penalising each additional parameter
begin{equation}
label{eqaic}
text{AIC}(p) = ln!left(frac{text{RSS}_{ur}}{T}right) + frac{2k}{T},
end{equation}
where $k = 2p + 1$ is the total number of estimated parameters in the unrestricted model. The criterion is evaluated for every candidate lag order from $p = 1$ to a ceiling of $p_{max} = 30$ trading days (approximately six trading weeks), and the lag order that minimises the AIC is selected for the $F$-test. This ceiling is deliberately conservative it is wide enough to reveal lead-lag relationships extending beyond the short horizons typically examined in daily-frequency studies, yet tight enough to avoid the parameter proliferation that a much larger window would invite, which the AIC penalty alone might not adequately discipline in a sample of this length.

It is worth noting explicitly what Granger causality does and does not establish. The test provides evidence of incremental predictive content in a forecasting sense lagged entropy values carry information about future returns that is not already captured by the returns' own lags. It does not establish structural or economic causation citep{granger1969}. The entropy indicator may lead returns because it captures the early stages of a distributional shift that subsequently manifests in realised price movements, or because both series respond to a common underlying driver with different speeds. The test is agnostic about the mechanism; it speaks only to the statistical predictability that an early warning system requires.


subsection{Internal Coherence of the Entropy Indicators}
label{subsecews_coherence}

Before interpreting the lead-lag results, it is useful to examine whether the three entropy components, full, left-tail, and right-tail, move together or exhibit distinct dynamics. This is assessed by computing the pairwise CCF among all three indicators. If the components share a common factor, their pairwise CCFs should peak near zero lag with high correlation. If they respond to different types of shocks with different timing, the pairwise CCFs may reveal non-trivial lead-lag structure among the entropy measures themselves.

The coherence test applied here follows the internal-consistency diagnostic used in composite-indicator construction citep{bujosa2013}. In that framework, a well-constructed composite leading indicator should have components that are internally coherent, meaning they move together contemporaneously ($h^ approx 0$) rather than leading or lagging one another. The expectation for the entropy indicators is similar but not identical the full-distribution entropy is by construction a function of both tails, so it should be contemporaneously correlated with each tail component. The left-tail and right-tail entropies, however, respond to different types of shocks, downside and upside respectively, and need not be correlated with each other.


subsection{Results}
label{subsecews_results}

The early warning battery is applied to the three rolling-window entropy indicators (full, left-tail, and right-tail KL divergence against the Normal baseline) computed at $h = 5$ over the out-of-sample period from June 2012 to April 2026. After first-differencing the entropy series, the paired sample used for the cross-correlation and Granger analyses comprises 2{,}167 aligned daily observations (the Brent return entering in levels).

subsubsection{Cross-Correlation Function Results}

All three entropy indicators lead Brent returns the peak cross-correlation falls
at a strictly positive lag in every case and is negative throughout. The
right-tail entropy peaks earliest and strongest ($h^ = +6$, $r = -0.250$), the
full-distribution entropy at $h^ = +14$ ($r = -0.229$), and the left-tail
entropy latest and weakest ($h^ = +16$, $r = -0.110$); all exceed the Bartlett
95 percent band of $pm 0.042$ in absolute value.footnote{Because each entropy
indicator is itself computed from a density forecast targeted $h = 5$ days ahead,
part of the measured lead is mechanical rather than discovered. The
cross-correlation and Granger exercises are therefore intended to confirm that
this anticipatory content is statistically detectable in realised returns, not to
claim it was found by accident.} The common negative sign is economically
coherent rising distributional stress precedes weaker returns, the same channel
that underlies the GaR{} paradigm. The association is modest in magnitude,
however---the squared peak correlations imply that only about one to six percent
of return variation is linearly linked to the leading signal (6.3% right tail,
5.2% full, 1.2% left).

That all three peaks occur at $h^  0$ is the central finding over this sample
the entropy measures behave as leading, not coincident, indicators. The CCF is
only bivariate, though, and cannot rule out a common third factor driving both
series; the Granger test in the next subsection controls for this in part by
conditioning on the returns' own history.

subsubsection{Granger Causality Results}

ceiling at $p_{max} = 30$ lags, the criterion selects lag orders of 16 to 22
trading days across the three indicators.

subsection{Synthesis and Implications}
label{subsecews_synthesis}

The combined evidence from the Cross-Correlation Function and the Granger causality test is consistent with the hypothesis that the entropy measures derived from the OaR{} framework contain early warning information about oil price movements over the sample studied. The main observations are as follows. All three entropy indicators exhibit peak cross-correlations with Brent returns at positive lags, ranging from 6 to 16 trading days, so that over this sample they behave as leading rather than contemporaneous diagnostics. The peak correlations are negative throughout, meaning that a rise in distributional stress tends to precede weaker returns, and although the associated lead times are non-trivial the implied linear explanatory power is modest, with squared peak correlations between roughly one and six percent. The Granger causality tests indicate that the predictive content of the entropy indicators is incremental it is not replicable by the returns' own autoregressive structure alone. The right-tail entropy tends to provide the strongest and fastest signal, consistent with the abrupt, event-driven nature of supply-side shocks in crude oil markets, while the left-tail entropy provides a weaker but still statistically significant signal, consistent with the slower propagation of demand-side risks. Finally, the internal coherence analysis indicates that the left-tail and right-tail entropies respond to largely distinct phenomena and carry independent information about different types of oil market risk.

From a practical perspective, the system suggests the following operational logic over the period examined, subject to the caveat that the measured associations, while statistically detectable, are individually weak. A risk manager monitoring the OaR{} dashboard observes the daily rolling entropy series decomposed by tail. A rise in right-tail entropy indicates that the conditional distribution is developing unusually heavy upside dispersion, potentially driven by geopolitical escalation or supply tightening, and this elevated stress has tended, over this sample, to precede softer realised returns within roughly one to one and a half trading weeks. A rise in left-tail entropy indicates the accumulation of downside tail risk, potentially associated with weakening demand conditions or an appreciating dollar, with a somewhat longer and weaker lead. The full-distribution entropy, which aggregates both tails, provides the broadest signal. These lead times are long enough to be of potential use in risk management, informing decisions such as adjusting hedge ratios, recalibrating margin requirements, or revising scenario assumptions for stress testing; given the modest effect sizes, however, the indicator is best read as one input among several rather than a standalone trigger. It nonetheless extends the distributional analysis of the OaR{} framework from a purely retrospective diagnostic toward a forward-looking surveillance tool.

section{Conclusion}
label{secconclusion}

This thesis built and evaluated OaR{}, a probabilistic tail-risk framework for
daily Brent crude returns that adapts the GaR{} paradigm of citet{adrian2019}
to the energy domain quantile regressions on geopolitical and macroeconomic
conditioning variables, estimated by direct multi-step forecasting; a continuous
Johnson $S_U$ conditional density fitted by Minimum Distance Estimation at each
origin; and Value-at-Risk, Expected Shortfall, Kullback--Leibler divergence, and
skewness read from that density. Across the out-of-sample period the fitted
densities were statistically adequate (the PIT did not reject uniformity), all
three specifications passed the Kupiec coverage test at roughly 97--98 percent,
and none dominated on predictive score, so the parsimonious standard model was
retained with the CAViaR variants kept as shorter-horizon complements.

The central conclusion is that these distributional outputs can be assembled into
a genuine early warning system. The entropy indicator, the tail-decomposed
Kullback--Leibler divergence of the conditional density, led Brent returns by 6
to 16 trading days over the sample and Granger-caused them at the 1 percent level,
so the framework does not merely describe risk after the fact but anticipates it.
The signal responds to both sources of tail activity that matter in this market
the geopolitical shocks that enter through the conditioning variables, and the
broader movements in market volatility, including speculative positioning and
directional-dispersion dynamics, that reshape the conditional density whatever
their origin. That this anticipatory content survives at emph{daily} frequency,
and in an asset as volatile as crude oil, is the result that matters it is
precisely in a high-frequency, high-volatility setting that the backward-looking,
Gaussian risk measures of standard practice are least reliable and an early,
tail-specific warning is most valuable. The lead times observed here are long
enough to be operationally meaningful, giving a risk manager a window to adjust
hedges and margins before stress is realised rather than after.

section{Optimisation Method and Hypotheses}
label{secappendix_optim}

In standard OLS, because the squared loss function creates a smooth parabola, taking the derivative and setting it to zero yields a closed-form algebraic solution $hatbeta = (X^top X)^{-1}X^top Y$. However, the absolute value in the check function creates a sharp ``V'' shape at zero. This non-differentiable corner means that standard first-order conditions cannot be applied. Instead, the optimisation is transformed into a Linear Programming (LP) problem.

To eliminate the absolute piecewise nature of the function, every residual is split into two strictly non-negative directional buckets $u_i^+$ (the under-prediction bucket, measuring the magnitude by which the actual value exceeded the prediction) and $u_i^-$ (the over-prediction bucket, measuring the magnitude by which the prediction exceeded the actual value). Every residual can now be expressed as a linear combination $u_i = u_i^+ - u_i^-$. Substituting this into the regression equation ($y_i - x_i^topbeta = u_i^+ - u_i^-$) and rearranging defines the structural constraints for the optimisation algorithm
begin{equation}
x_i^top beta + u_i^+ - u_i^- = y_i.
label{eqlp_constraint}
end{equation}
Applying this transformation removes the absolute value operator from the objective function. The minimisation problem is expressed in a standard primal Linear Programming format
begin{equation}
min_{beta,,u^+,,u^-};sum_{i=1}^{n}bigl[tau,u_i^+ + (1 - tau),u_i^-bigr]quad text{subject to}quad x_i^top beta + u_i^+ - u_i^- = y_i,quad u_i^+,,u_i^- geq 0.
label{eqlp}
end{equation}
By formulating the problem this way, statistical software can solve the regression using efficient LP algorithms, primarily the Barrodale--Roberts modified simplex algorithm citep{barrodale1973} for smaller datasets, which searches for the minimum along the geometric edges of the data intersections, or Frisch--Newton interior-point methods citep{portnoy1997} for large datasets, which cut through the feasible space using a logarithmic barrier to ensure positivity.

For $hatbeta_tau$ to be a consistent estimator, the model relies on several core hypotheses. Linearity of conditional quantiles requires that the conditional quantile has a linear relationship with the regressors, such that $Q_tau(Y mid X) = X^topbeta(tau)$. Strict exogeneity requires that the conditional quantile of the error term, given $X$, is zero $Q_tau(u mid X) = 0$. No perfect multicollinearity requires that the matrix $mathbb{E}[XX^top]$ is finite, positive-definite, and invertible. Positive density requires that the conditional density function of the error term around zero is strictly positive and finite ($0  f_{uX}(0 mid X)  infty$). Because precision in quantile regression depends on the density of observations near the estimated quantile, this condition ensures the estimator is well-identified.

section{Quantile Regression Diagnostics Full Technical Development}
label{secappendix_diagnostics}

This appendix presents the methodological foundation of each diagnostic test applied to the specification. For each test, the statistical framework, null hypothesis, test statistic, and asymptotic distribution are stated. Throughout, $rho_tau(u) = u,[tau - mathbf{1}(u  0)]$ denotes the check (pinball) function introduced in Section~ref{subsecqr}, and $k$ denotes the number of slope parameters in the specification.

subsection{Goodness-of-Fit Koenker--Machado $R^1(tau)$}
label{subsecr1}

Unlike Ordinary Least Squares, quantile regression does not minimise a quadratic objective and therefore possesses no natural coefficient of determination. The local goodness-of-fit measure introduced by citet{koenker1999} resolves this by defining a pseudo-$R^2$ as the proportional reduction in the asymmetrically weighted sum of absolute errors relative to an intercept-only null model
begin{equation}
R^1(tau) = 1 - frac{displaystylesum_{t=1}^{T}rho_tau!left(y_t - x_t'hatbeta(tau)right)}{displaystylesum_{t=1}^{T}rho_tau!left(y_t - hatxi_tauright)},
label{eqr1}
end{equation}
where the numerator is the optimised value of the check-loss objective for the full specification and the denominator is the same objective evaluated at the intercept-only model, whose solution $hatxi_tau$ is the unconditional sample $tau$-quantile. Because both sums are strictly non-negative and the full model can only reduce the objective relative to the restricted one, the metric is bounded in $[0,1]$ and is interpreted locally at each $tau$ a value of $R^1(tau) = 0.10$ at $tau = 0.05$ indicates that the regressors reduce the weighted objective by ten percent relative to the unconditional quantile in that tail. citet{koenker1999} establish that the statistic is consistent and that, under standard regularity conditions,
begin{equation}
T cdot V(tau) cdot R^1(tau) xrightarrow{d} chi^2_k,
label{eqr1_dist}
end{equation}
where $k$ is the number of slope parameters and $V(tau)$ is a nuisance scale that depends on the sparsity of the error distribution at $tau$, that is, on the reciprocal of the conditional error density evaluated at the $tau$-quantile. The dependence on sparsity is the same feature that governs the asymptotic variance of the quantile-regression coefficients themselves precision deteriorates wherever the data are thin around the fitted quantile.

subsection{Wald Test for Equality of Coefficients across Quantiles}
label{subsecwald}

A foundational identifying assumption of the OaR{} framework is that the effect of the conditioning variables on the return distribution is heterogeneous across quantiles. If the slope coefficients were identical for every $tau$, the distribution would shift in parallel and quantile regression would collapse to a reparameterisation of OLS. The Wald test formalises this by testing coefficient constancy across the $m$ estimated quantiles jointly citep{koenker1978}. Let
begin{equation}
hatzeta = bigl(hatbeta(tau_1)',, hatbeta(tau_2)',, ldots,, hatbeta(tau_m)'bigr)'
label{eqwald_stack}
end{equation}
be the $mp times 1$ stacked parameter vector across all $m$ quantiles, where $p$ is the dimension of $beta$ at each quantile. A full-rank $q times mp$ restriction matrix $R$ is constructed to extract the relevant slope coefficients and impose equality across quantile pairs, so that the null reads $H_0! Rzeta = r$, with $r$ the $q times 1$ vector of hypothesised values (typically zero for difference restrictions). The Wald statistic is
begin{equation}
T_n = n,(Rhatzeta - r)^top!left[R,hat{V}_n,R^topright]^{-1}!(Rhatzeta - r) xrightarrow{d} chi^2_q,
label{eqwald}
end{equation}
where $hat{V}_n$ is the joint asymptotic covariance matrix of $hatzeta$. Because the parameters at different quantiles are estimated from the same time series, their sampling errors are correlated across quantiles, so the off-diagonal blocks of $hat{V}_n$ are non-zero and cannot be ignored. Analytical sparsity estimators are moreover unreliable in the presence of temporal dependence. Accordingly, $hat{V}_n$ is constructed by a moving block bootstrap contiguous blocks of observations of length $l$, chosen to span the autocorrelation horizon of the data, are resampled, and the full quantile system is re-estimated on each replication, so that the resampled blocks preserve the serial dependence that an i.i.d. bootstrap would destroy.

subsection{Quantile Crossing Inspection}
label{subseccrossing}

A necessary condition for any valid conditional distribution function is that the conditional quantile function be monotonically non-decreasing in $tau$. Formally, for any two levels $tau_1  tau_2$ and any observation $t$,
begin{equation}
hat{q}_t(tau_1) leq hat{q}_t(tau_2) qquad forall, t in {1, ldots, T}.
label{eqcrossing}
end{equation}
Violations of this condition, known as quantile crossings, arise when the estimated linear quantile planes intersect within the covariate support, typically as a consequence of over-parameterisation, collinear regressors, or an insufficient number of observations relative to the complexity of the model. Crossings are detected by computing, for each observation $t$, the ordered sequence of fitted values across all targeted quantiles and checking for any inversion of that ordering. The proportion of crossing observations and the magnitude of the maximal inversion are reported as informal diagnostics; a specification with systematic crossings in the interior of the covariate support is treated as misspecified.

subsection{Stationarity Quantile Autoregressive Unit Root Test}
label{subsecqar}

Standard unit-root tests evaluate the null of a unit root against the alternative of global mean reversion, implicitly assuming a single autoregressive parameter across the whole distribution. citet{koenker2004} relax this by permitting quantile-specific autoregressive dynamics, so that a series may be stationary at some quantiles while behaving near a unit root at others. The test is implemented through the auxiliary error-correction regression
begin{equation}
Delta y_t = mu(tau) + rho(tau),y_{t-1} + sum_{j=1}^{p}gamma_j(tau),Delta y_{t-j} + epsilon_t(tau),
label{eqqar}
end{equation}
where the lag order $p$ is selected by an information criterion applied to the augmented Dickey--Fuller pre-regression. The null $H_0! rho(tau) = 0$ posits a quantile-specific unit root and is tested against the one-sided alternative $H_1! rho(tau)  0$. The statistic $t_{text{QAR}}(tau) = hatrho(tau)widehat{operatorname{se}}(hatrho(tau))$ is obtained from the quantile objective and does not follow a standard normal distribution under the null. citet{koenker2004} show that its limiting distribution is
begin{equation}
t_{text{QAR}}(tau) xrightarrow{d} frac{f!left(F^{-1}(tau)right)}{sqrt{tau(1-tau)}}cdot frac{displaystyleint_0^1 W(s),dW(s)}{left(displaystyleint_0^1 W(s)^2,dsright)^{12}},
label{eqqar_dist}
end{equation}
where $W(s)$ is a standard Brownian motion on $[0,1]$, $f$ is the marginal density of the innovations, and $F^{-1}(tau)$ is the corresponding population quantile. The right-hand side is the standard Dickey--Fuller functional scaled by the sparsity factor $f(F^{-1}(tau))sqrt{tau(1-tau)}$, which varies with $tau$ and renders the ordinary Dickey--Fuller critical values inapplicable. Quantile-specific critical values are therefore obtained by simulation following citet{koenker2004}.

subsection{Dynamic Quantile Test}
label{subsecdq_app}

The Dynamic Quantile test of citet{engle2004} evaluates whether the sequence of quantile violations behaves as a martingale difference sequence, i.e. whether breaches are unpredictable from past information. The centred hit sequence is
begin{equation}
text{Hit}_t(tau) = mathbf{1}!left(y_t  hat{q}_t(tau)right) - tau,
label{eqdq_hit}
end{equation}
which has mean zero by construction under correct specification. The null hypothesis is
begin{equation}
H_0! ; mathbb{E}!left[text{Hit}_t(tau)mid Omega_{t-1}right] = 0,
label{eqdq_null_app}
end{equation}
so that the violation process is orthogonal to all past information $Omega_{t-1}$. To test this, the hits are projected by OLS onto an instrument matrix $Z_t$ comprising a constant, $L$ lagged hits $text{Hit}_{t-1}(tau), ldots, text{Hit}_{t-L}(tau)$, and the lagged fitted quantile $hat{q}_{t-1}(tau)$
begin{equation}
text{Hit}_t(tau) = z_t'delta + eta_t.
label{eqdq_proj}
end{equation}
citet{engle2004} show that under the null the Wald form of the statistic,
begin{equation}
DQ(tau) = frac{text{Hit}'Z,(Z'Z)^{-1}Z'text{Hit}}{tau(1-tau)} xrightarrow{d} chi^2_q,
label{eqdq_stat}
end{equation}
is asymptotically chi-squared with $q$ degrees of freedom equal to the column dimension of $Z$. The asymptotically equivalent $N cdot R^2$ form of the Lagrange-multiplier statistic converges to the same distribution and is reported for comparability. The test is applied separately at each targeted quantile; a joint test across quantiles is constructed by stacking the instrument matrices and adjusting the degrees of freedom accordingly. As explained in Section~ref{subsecdq_caviar}, the CAViaR specifications are designed to pass this test by construction, since their regressors are built precisely from the lagged breach information that appears in $Z_t$.

section{Minimum Distance Estimator}
label{secmed}


Two algorithmic choices materially affect the reliability of the solution. First, the initial guess $Theta_0$ strongly influences the speed and likelihood of convergence. The initialisation sets $xi$ at the predicted median $hat{q}_{t+h}(0.50)$, $lambda$ at the interquartile range $hat{q}_{t+h}(0.75) - hat{q}_{t+h}(0.25)$, and $gamma$ and $delta$ at zero and one, respectively. This encodes a symmetric, moderately heavy-tailed prior centred on the regression's median forecast, a sensible starting point that the optimiser then refines as it absorbs the asymmetry embedded in the outer quantiles. Second, convergence tolerances must be tight enough to produce an accurate fit but loose enough to avoid excessive iterations on nearly flat regions of the objective (sum-of-squares) surface; a function tolerance of $10^{-10}$ has proven adequate across the full out-of-sample experiment.footnote{The objective in eqref{eqmde} weights the $K=7$ quantile residuals equally in an unweighted $L_2$ sum. Equal weighting is deliberate it treats the fit to each targeted quantile as equally informative about the shape of the density and avoids importing an ad hoc weighting scheme that would itself require justification. Because the problem matches $K=7$ target quantiles to a four-parameter family, it is over-identified with three residual degrees of freedom, so the fit is a genuine estimation rather than exact interpolation; the fitted density is not forced through every point and the residual pattern is itself a diagnostic of misspecification. A density- or spacing-weighted variant is a natural robustness check but is not pursued here.}

The procedure is repeated independently for every forecast origin in the rolling window, yielding a time series of fitted parameter vectors ${hatTheta_t}_{t in W}$ and, through them, a time series of continuous conditional densities. Each density is anchored to the information set available at its forecast origin it inherits the conditioning embedded in the quantile forecasts and imposes no cross-sectional pooling across dates. The result is a sequence of distributions that evolves with the geopolitical and macroeconomic environment, fattening its left tail when the Geopolitical Risk Index spikes and narrowing it when conditions normalise, which is the object the OaR{} framework requires. An in-sample example of a fitted conditional density is displayed as a three-dimensional surface in Figure 3.


section{The Johnson texorpdfstring{$S_U$}{SU} Distribution}
label{secappendix_jsu}

The parametric family used to interpolate the quantile skeleton in
Section~ref{secmethodology} must satisfy two requirements. First, it must be
flexible enough to accommodate the stylised facts of crude oil returns excess
kurtosis, asymmetric tails, and time-varying skewness whose direction shifts as
geopolitical conditions evolve. When a supply disruption threatens, the right
tail of the return distribution fattens as the market prices in the possibility
of a sharp price spike; when a demand collapse materialises, the left tail
fattens instead. A distribution that imposes symmetry, or that controls tail
weight through a single parameter, cannot track these asymmetric rotations.
Second, its cumulative distribution function must admit a closed-form inverse,
because the estimation procedure evaluates the theoretical quantile function
many thousands of times.

The Johnson $S_U$ distribution satisfies both. A random variable $Y$ follows a
Johnson $S_U$ if
begin{equation}
Y = xi + lambda sinh!left(frac{Z - gamma}{delta}right),quad Z sim mathcal{N}(0,1),
label{eqjsu}
end{equation}
where $xi$ is the location parameter, $lambda  0$ is the scale, $gamma$
governs skewness, and $delta  0$ governs tail weight. The quantile function
follows by replacing $Z$ with $Phi^{-1}(p)$
begin{equation}
Q(p) = xi + lambda,sinh!left(frac{Phi^{-1}(p) - gamma}{delta}right).
label{eqjsu_quantile}
end{equation}
This closed-form quantile function is the decisive computational advantage it
evaluates rapidly, involves no numerical root-finding, and makes the Minimum
Distance Estimation step as fast as a simple least-squares problem.

The economic appeal of the $S_U$ family becomes clear when one considers the
alternative. The Generalised Error Distribution, widely used in GARCH modelling
citep{nelson1991}, controls tail weight through a single shape parameter but
imposes symmetry it cannot distinguish between a left-skewed distribution during
a demand collapse and a right-skewed one during a supply panic. The Johnson $S_U$
provides independent control over skewness and kurtosis through the two separate
shape parameters $gamma$ and $delta$. In the limit $gamma to 0$,
$delta to infty$, the $S_U$ nests the Gaussian as a special case, so that
tranquil periods are accommodated without forcing heavy tails where none exist.
The original GaR{} framework of citet{adrian2019} employs the Azzalini
skewed-$t$ distribution for this step; the $S_U$ is a more finance-oriented
alternative that has been used to model asset returns in portfolio management and
Value-at-Risk contexts, with comparable flexibility and a simpler
quantile-function inversion.

subsection{Kullback--Leibler Divergence Definition and Decomposition}
label{secappendix_kl}

Kullback--Leibler divergence citep{kullback1951} provides a global measure of
distributional distance. Given a conditional forecast density $p(x)$ and a
reference density $q(x)$,
begin{equation}
D_{text{KL}}(p ,, q) = int_{-infty}^{infty} p(x) ln!left(frac{p(x)}{q(x)}right) dx.
label{eqkl}
end{equation}
The divergence is always non-negative and equals zero if and only if the two
densities are identical. Its asymmetry is economically meaningful
$D_{text{KL}}(p ,, q)$ heavily penalises the reference density $q$ when it
assigns near-zero probability to events that the conditional density $p$
considers plausible---in risk-management terms, the dangerous scenario in which
the reference model says a large loss is essentially impossible while the
conditional forecast says it has non-negligible probability.

Through the lens of information theory citep{shannon1948}, the divergence is the
expected excess surprise when $q$ is used to describe a world actually governed
by $p$
begin{equation}
D_{text{KL}}(p ,, q) = mathbb{E}_p!left[-ln q(x)right] - mathbb{E}_p!left[-ln p(x)right].
label{eqkl_entropy}
end{equation}
The first term is the cross-entropy of $q$ under $p$; the second is the entropy
of $p$ itself. The divergence measures the extra information, in nats, needed to
describe the true state of the world when the wrong model is assumed.

Following citet{adrian2019}, the divergence is decomposed into left-tail,
centre, and right-tail components by restricting the integration domain. With
$tau = 0.95$ as the default tail mark (isolating the extreme 5 percent of each
tail, rather than the median split used in the original GaR{} framework, which
is better suited to quarterly GDP growth than to daily commodity returns),
begin{equation}
D_{text{KL}}^{text{Left}}(p ,, q) = int_{-infty}^{q_{1-tau}} p(x)ln!left(frac{p(x)}{q(x)}right) dx,
label{eqkl_left}
end{equation}
begin{equation}
D_{text{KL}}^{text{Right}}(p ,, q) = int_{q_tau}^{infty} p(x)ln!left(frac{p(x)}{q(x)}right) dx.
label{eqkl_right}
end{equation}
Two reference baselines are employed. The first is a Normal density calibrated to
the historical mean and standard deviation, whose divergence measures the
departure from Gaussianity and functions as an early warning a rapid increase
signals that the market's return distribution is moving away from the
well-behaved state in which traditional risk metrics are reliable.footnote{The
Kullback--Leibler divergence of a heavy-tailed $p$ against a Gaussian $q$ is
dominated by the region where $qto 0$, so this measure behaves primarily as a
detector of tail-fatness (excess kurtosis) rather than of location or mild
skewness; this is a feature for early warning, since tail-fattening is precisely
the object of interest, but it should be read as a kurtosis-sensitive signal
rather than a generic ``distance''. The tail integrals are evaluated on a finite
grid, and the truncation range is set wide enough that the reported divergences
are numerically stable across the sample.} The second is the unconditional
Johnson $S_U$ density fitted by Maximum Likelihood to the historical returns,
which is the baseline used in the original Vulnerable Growth framework and is
shown in Figure 11. This divergence is more demanding, because the
unconditional $S_U$ already accommodates skewness and heavy tails; a high
divergence against it signals a genuine shift in the conditional distribution,
not merely a departure from Gaussianity.

section{Out-of-Sample Forecast Evaluation Tests}
label{secappendix_oos}

This appendix documents every test used to evaluate the direct-forecasting models. Two conventions hold throughout. First, a hit (or breach) is defined under a unified directional rule a hit occurs whenever the realised value falls strictly below the forecast, $y_{t+h}  hat{y}_{t+ht}(tau)$, so that for any quantile the model should be breached exactly $tau times 100%$ of the time. Second, all coverage tests are run out-of-sample on a fixed 1{,}000-day rolling window, re-estimated at every forecast origin.

subsection{The Rolling-Window Experiment}
label{subsecrolling}

Every statistic is computed on genuinely out-of-sample forecasts generated by a fixed-width rolling window. For each forecast origin $t$ in the test span the model is trained on the 1{,}000 observations in the half-open interval $[t - 1000,, t)$, a single $h$-step-ahead prediction of $Y_{t+h}$ is produced, and the window rolls forward one day. Because the realised target $Y_{t+h}$ never enters the training window, there is no load-bearing look-ahead. The reported figures at $h = 5$, $tau = 0.95$ are averages over hundreds of origins 864 out-of-sample forecasts for the benchmark (2 January 2023 to 23 April 2026) and 517 for the CAViaR variants (2 January 2023 to 20 April 2026), the difference reflecting the extra rows consumed by constructing the breach regressors.

subsection{Pinball Loss and Scoring}
label{subsecpinball_app}

In conditional-mean forecasting the mean-squared error is the natural default, but symmetry is precisely the wrong property for a quantile, where over- and under-prediction must be penalised by different amounts. The asymmetric pinball (check) loss supplies that asymmetry
begin{equation}
L_tau(e) = begin{cases} tau,e & text{if } e geq 0, (tau - 1),e & text{if } e  0, end{cases}qquad e = y_{t+h} - hat{y}(tau),
label{eqpinball_app}
end{equation}
which is always non-negative and can be written compactly as $L_tau(e) = e,[tau - mathbf{1}(e  0)]$. A scoring rule is emph{strictly proper} if its expectation is minimised if and only if the issued forecast equals the true value of the targeted functional. The pinball loss is the unique (up to affine transformation) strictly proper scoring rule for a quantile citep{gneiting2007}, which is the formal counterpart of the statement that the quantile is an emph{elicitable} functional. The practical consequence is strong if model $A$ attains a lower out-of-sample pinball loss than model $B$ at quantile $tau$, then $A$ is a better estimator of that $tau$-quantile, and the comparison requires no distributional assumption. This is what licenses ranking competing specifications directly by their realised loss.

subsection{Kupiec Proportion-of-Failures Test}
label{subseckupiec_app}

Before considering the timing of breaches, the simplest question is whether the $tau$-quantile forecast is breached with the correct emph{frequency}. The Kupiec proportion-of-failures test citep{kupiec1995} checks whether the empirical hit rate matches the nominal level; it is unconditional because it judges frequency alone and is silent about clustering. Let $T$ be the number of out-of-sample forecasts and $x$ the number of hits, with empirical rate $hatpi = xT$. Under the null the breach indicator is Bernoulli with the nominal probability, so
begin{equation}
H_0! ; mathbb{E}[I_{t+h}] = tau qquad text{vs.} qquad H_1! ; mathbb{E}[I_{t+h}] neq tau,
label{eqkupiec_hyp}
end{equation}
and the likelihood-ratio statistic contrasts the restricted likelihood (probability fixed at the nominal level) with the unrestricted one (probability free at $hatpi$)
begin{equation}
LR_{UC} = -2ln!left[frac{(1 - tau)^{T-x},tau^x}{(1 - hatpi)^{T-x},hatpi^x}right] xrightarrow{d} chi^2_1.
label{eqkupiec_app}
end{equation}
By Wilks' theorem the statistic is asymptotically chi-squared with degrees of freedom equal to the difference in the number of free parameters between the two likelihoods, here one (the free rate) minus zero (the fixed target), hence a single degree of freedom. Because the hit count is binomial, the central limit theorem also supplies a normal-approximation band around the nominal rate, $tau pm zsqrt{tau(1-tau)T}$, which is the envelope drawn in the coverage-convergence plots. As discussed in the main text, the overlapping structure of the $h$-step direct forecasts inflates the variance of the breach count but not its mean, so this unconditional-frequency assessment remains valid whereas a naive independence-based conditional-coverage test would be size-distorted; the latter is therefore not reported.

subsection{Diebold--Mariano Test of Equal Predictive Accuracy}
label{subsecdm_app}

The coverage test certifies each model in isolation; the Diebold--Mariano test citep{diebold1995} compares two models head-to-head. Let $L_{1,t}$ and $L_{2,t}$ be the element-wise tick losses of two competing forecasts and $d_t = L_{1,t} - L_{2,t}$ their differential. The null of equal predictive accuracy is $H_0! mathbb{E}[d_t] = 0$. The statistic standardises the mean differential by a heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent (HAC) long-run variance
begin{equation}
DM = frac{bar{d}}{sqrt{widehat{LRV}P}},qquad widehat{LRV} = gamma_0 + 2sum_{k=1}^{h-1}gamma_k ;xrightarrow{d}; t_{P-1},
label{eqdm}
end{equation}
where $gamma_k$ is the sample autocovariance of $d_t$ at lag $k$ and $P$ is the number of paired forecast origins. Two implementation choices follow the theory. The HAC bandwidth is set to $h - 1$, because the errors of an $h$-step direct forecast follow an MA$(h-1)$ process and therefore have exactly $h-1$ non-zero autocovariances; truncating the kernel there is efficient and consistent. The reference distribution is taken as Student-$t$ with $P - 1$ degrees of freedom rather than the standard normal, a small-sample correction. A negative and significant $bar{d}$ indicates that the first model carries the lower average loss; a value insignificantly different from zero indicates that the two forecasts are statistically indistinguishable in accuracy, in which case parsimony is the natural tie-breaker.
